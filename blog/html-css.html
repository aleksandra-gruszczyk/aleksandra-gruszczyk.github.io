CSS Box Model

What is the difference between Margin, Border, and Padding?

While these terms sound like something more related to fashion industry than programming, they are actually very helpful in visualising the look of the content on your website. They are all elements of a concept called the CSS Box Model.

The CSS Box Model is a visualisation of a set of layers surrounding every HTML element. Imagine an egg, an ogre, or an onion: yes, LAYERS!

At the core of the CSS Box Model is the content. The text and/or images is always at the center of your box, just like the yolk sits at the center of the egg. Surrounding the content is a layer of padding.

Padding is essentially a protective layer around your content that wraps it up on all sides so that the content doesn't touch the borders of its space - just like the white surrounding the yolk.

Border is the impregnable layer that separates your snuggly padded content from the outside world: think of the egg shell. You can make it visible or not, thick or thin - but it's always there.

Margin is the area that lies beyond the border. Imagine a lot of eggs! You can put them all together, risking smashing them all into scrambled eggs, or you can separate them from each other, putting each one in their own neat little containers.

For margins (yellow) and padding (purple), you can choose which side should or should not have them, and how thick: you can choose to have margins on all sides, as I did, or you can choose to have them on any side, or no side at all.

CSS is nice enough to use the clockwork directions to indicate the sequence of directions: so the first slot is for "up," the second for "right,", the third for "bottom," and the fourth for "left." But if you write only one value, it will assume you want it on all sides.
